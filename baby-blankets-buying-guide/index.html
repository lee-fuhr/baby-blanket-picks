How to choose a baby blankets

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose a receiving blanket or swaddle blanket by first deciding whether you want a simple square for general use (receiving) or a shaped, stay-put wrap for sleep (swaddle). Then match the fabric to your climate and baby’s sensitivity: lightweight muslin for warmth without overheating, cotton knit for stretch, or bamboo for softness. Prioritize safety by keeping loose blankets out of the crib and following the manufacturer’s weight limits on swaddle products.

Receiving blanket vs. swaddle blanket: what’s the difference?

A receiving blanket is a large (often 30 by 30 inches or larger) square of lightweight fabric. It’s a multi-purpose workhorse: you use it to wrap your baby loosely, as a burp cloth, a nursing cover, a stroller shade, or a play mat. It is not designed to stay tightly wrapped on its own. A swaddle blanket, on the other hand, is purpose-built for the hip-healthy, snug wrapping that helps newborns sleep. Some are simply larger receiving squares with instructions; others are shaped sacks with Velcro or zip closures that make wrapping foolproof. The key difference is that a swaddle is meant to hold the baby firmly without coming undone, limiting the startle reflex. For a newborn, you will likely want both: a few receiving blankets for everyday messes and warmth, and at least one purpose-made swaddle (or a very large muslin square you can learn to fold) for sleep.

What fabric is best for baby blankets?

Cotton muslin is the gold standard for swaddling because it is breathable, lightly stretchy, and softens with washing. Brands like Aden + Anais have popularized this fabric for good reason: it reduces overheating risk while still providing warmth. For cooler climates or heavier swaddles, cotton flannel or jersey knit offer more weight and stretch, but they are less airy. Bamboo rayon has become popular for its silky softness and temperature regulation, though it tends to be pricier. Wool or fleece blankets are best used as outer layers for stroller rides, not for sleep, because they trap heat too effectively. Whatever you choose, look for fabric that is pre-washed (many muslin blankets shrink). Avoid anything with loose weaves or long loops that could catch tiny fingers. And remember: babies cannot regulate their body temperature well, so a breathable fabric is safer than a heavy one.

How many blankets do you really need for a newborn?

Plan for at least four to six receiving blankets if you are using them for burping, spills, and general layering. If you plan to swaddle for sleep, you will want three to four swaddle-specific blankets so you always have a clean one in the rotation while others are washing. Many parents find that they use two distinct sizes: a large square (40 inches or more) for swaddling, and smaller squares (30 inches) for everything else. Having too few can lead to laundry desperation; having too many means you will likely stop using them after three months when the baby starts rolling.

Safety first: what to look for in a sleep swaddle

The safest swaddle is one that cannot come loose and cover your baby’s face. That means either a well-folded square that stays snug (check for fabric creeping up) or a product with secure closures. Swallow-style swaddles with Velcro, zippers, or snaps are generally safer for beginners because they eliminate loose wraps. Always follow the weight and size guidelines on the product: a swaddle that is too big will loosen, and one too small can restrict breathing or hip movement. Once your baby shows signs of rolling (usually by eight weeks or earlier), stop swaddling entirely and switch to a sleep sack. A rolled baby cannot push up or turn if his arms are pinned. Room temperature also matters: keep the nursery between 68 and 72 degrees Fahrenheit, and dress your baby in a onesie under the swaddle. Overheating is a known SIDS risk, so check your baby’s neck or chest for sweat rather than relying on cold hands.

What size and shape works best for swaddling?

For square swaddle blankets, bigger is almost always better. A 40-by-40-inch or 47-by-47-inch square gives you enough fabric to wrap a newborn securely and still have room to grow for a few weeks. Smaller squares (30 inches) are fine for receiving but frustrating for a tight swaddle. If you prefer a shaped swaddle, look for one with a generous fit around the legs for healthy hip development. Many brands now offer adjustable fasteners so you can customize the snugness. Some come with a bottom zipper for easy diaper changes, which is a huge convenience in the middle of the night. Also consider whether you want arms-up or arms-down freedom: some babies prefer being swaddled with hands near their face (which helps self-soothing), so look for swaddles that allow that option.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a receiving blanket to swaddle my baby for sleep?

Yes, many parents use large receiving blankets as swaddles, but you must fold them correctly and ensure the wrap does not come loose. For safety, use a blanket at least 40 inches square and learn a proper hip-healthy swaddle fold. If you are worried about looseness, a purpose-made swaddle with Velcro or a zipper is safer.

When should I stop swaddling my baby?

Stop swaddling as soon as your baby shows signs of rolling over, usually around 8 weeks, but sometimes earlier. A swaddled baby who rolls onto their stomach cannot push up or turn their head, which is a SIDS risk. Transition to a sleep sack or a wearable blanket at that point.

How do I know if my baby is too warm in a swaddle?

Check the back of your baby’s neck or their chest: if that area is sweaty or clammy, they are too warm. Also look for flushed cheeks, rapid breathing, or fussiness. Adjust the room temperature or switch to a lighter fabric like muslin. Never put a hat on your baby indoors while swaddled.

Are bamboo baby blankets worth the higher price?

Bamboo blankets are extremely soft, naturally breathable, and often more durable after many washes than cotton muslin. They are a good choice if your baby has sensitive skin or you live in a warm climate. However, they cost more, and many cotton muslin options perform just as well for most families.

How many swaddle blankets do I need specifically for sleep?

Most parents find three to four swaddle blankets sufficient. You will go through one or two per night if there are spit-up or diaper leaks, and you want to have clean ones ready while the others are in the laundry. If you are using a shaped swaddle with removable inserts, you may need fewer because they can be wiped clean.

Can I use a fleece blanket as a swaddle?

Fleece is too warm for indoor swaddling unless the room is very cold (below 68 degrees). It is also not breathable, which increases overheating risk. Fleece blankets are fine as an outer layer for stroller walks, but for sleep, stick to cotton, muslin, or bamboo.

In shortThe best blanket for your baby is one that balances safety, comfort, and your own confidence. Start with a few good-quality muslin squares or a well-reviewed swaddle sack, pay attention to how your baby responds, and do not be afraid to switch fabrics or styles. If the blanket stays put and your baby sleeps well, you have found the right one.
