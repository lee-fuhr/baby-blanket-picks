Baby Blankets questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Receiving blankets are multipurpose, smaller squares for burping, tummy time, or light warmth, while swaddle blankets are larger, typically stretchy or muslin, designed specifically for snug, safe wrapping. For a newborn, aim for at least 4–6 receiving blankets and 3–4 swaddle blankets to handle spit-ups, laundry, and nighttime swaddling rotations.

What is the difference between a receiving blanket and a swaddle blanket?

A receiving blanket is a general-purpose square of fabric, usually around 30 by 30 inches, made from cotton flannel, jersey knit, or lightweight muslin. It’s used for burping, as a nursing cover, a stroller blanket, or a clean surface for diaper changes. A swaddle blanket is larger, often 47 by 47 inches, and designed with enough fabric to wrap around a baby’s arms and torso snugly. Many swaddle blankets are made from stretchy or extra-soft muslin that allows a secure, yet breathable, wrap. Swaddle blankets often have a bit of stretch or a specific weave that helps the wrap hold without needing tight tucking. Receiving blankets can be used for swaddling in a pinch, but their smaller size makes a secure wrap harder to achieve with a growing baby. The key difference is size and intended use: receiving is utility, swaddling is sleep safety.

How many baby blankets do I need for a newborn?

Most parents find that having at least 4 to 6 receiving blankets is ideal. They get dirty quickly, from spit-up, drool, or diaper leaks, and having extras means you’re not doing laundry every single day. For swaddle blankets, 3 to 4 is a comfortable rotation: one in use, one in the wash, and one backup. Babies sometimes soak through a swaddle overnight, and you’ll want a clean one ready. If you live in a colder climate or plan to use blankets for stroller walks, you may want a few more. Keep in mind that newborns outgrow the swaddle stage by about 2–4 months, so you don’t need a massive stash of swaddle-specific blankets. Receiving blankets stay useful well into toddlerhood for all kinds of messes.

What size blanket should I get for a newborn?

For receiving blankets, the standard size is around 30 by 30 inches. That’s big enough to lay over a changing pad or drape over your shoulder, but not so large that it becomes a tangle hazard. For swaddling, look for squares that are at least 45 by 45 inches. A smaller swaddle blanket will become difficult to wrap securely once your baby reaches double-digit pounds. Some brands make oversized swaddle blankets, up to 50 by 50 inches, which give extra fabric for a deep, hospital-style wrap. If you plan to use the blanket for other purposes like a nursing cover, a larger size also works. Just make sure it’s not so large that it feels heavy or hard to handle during a midnight change.

What materials are best for baby blankets?

Cotton muslin is the most popular choice for swaddle blankets because it’s lightweight, breathable, and becomes softer with each wash. It has an open weave that reduces overheating risk. For receiving blankets, cotton flannel is a common workhorse, it’s warm but not too thick, and it absorbs moisture well. Jersey knit (like soft T-shirt material) is another favorite because it has a bit of stretch and is very gentle on sensitive skin. Avoid heavy fleece or thick acrylic knits for newborns, as they can trap heat. Also steer clear of blankets with loose threading or decorative tassels that could pose a choking hazard. Look for Oeko-Tex certified or GOTS organic labels if you’re concerned about dyes and chemicals. The safest bets are natural, breathable fibers that wash well and hold up to repeated laundering.

Are muslin blankets good for swaddling?

Yes, muslin is one of the best materials for swaddling. Its open, gauzy weave allows air to circulate, which helps regulate your baby’s temperature and lowers the risk of overheating. The fabric also gets softer over time, making repeated washes a benefit rather than a drawback. Many hospital nurseries and pediatricians recommend muslin swaddle blankets precisely because they strike a good balance between snugness and breathability. One thing to note: pure muslin has little to no stretch. That means you need to wrap it fairly tightly to create a secure swaddle, and the corners can be a bit bulky under a baby’s chin. Some parents prefer a cotton-blend muslin with a touch of elastane for a little give. Either way, muslin remains a top choice because it’s lightweight enough for warm weather yet layers well for cooler nights.

How do I safely swaddle my baby?

The goal of a safe swaddle is to keep your baby snug without restricting hip movement or covering the face. Place the blanket on a flat surface with one corner folded down. Lay your baby face-up on the blanket with their neck at the fold. Pull one side of the blanket across their body, tuck it under their back, then bring the bottom corner up over their feet. Finally, bring the remaining side across and tuck it under, leaving the face and head uncovered. Ensure the swaddle is tight enough that the baby cannot wiggle the blanket loose, but loose enough that you can slide two fingers between the blanket and their chest. The hips should have room to bend outwards (the “frog leg” position) to prevent hip dysplasia. Stop swaddling as soon as your baby shows signs of rolling over, which can happen as early as 8 weeks. At that point, switch to a sleep sack or transition swaddle.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a receiving blanket as a swaddle blanket?

You can in a pinch, but it’s not ideal. Standard receiving blankets are too small to create a secure wrap for most babies beyond the first few weeks. A swaddle blanket’s larger size and often stretchy weave make it much easier to achieve a safe, snug wrap that stays in place.

How many swaddle blankets should I have?

Three to four swaddle blankets is a good number for most families. You will want one in use, one in the wash, and one or two backups for overnight leaks or spit-up. Since swaddle use only lasts a few months, it’s better not to overbuy.

Are fleece blankets safe for newborns?

Fleece blankets can cause overheating in newborns because they trap heat and do not breathe as well as cotton or muslin. If you use fleece, choose a very lightweight version and keep the room temperature on the cooler side. Many pediatricians recommend sticking to breathable fabrics for sleep-time blankets.

When should I stop swaddling my baby?

Stop swaddling as soon as your baby shows signs of trying to roll over, which can happen as early as 8 weeks. Continuing to swaddle after rolling starts increases the risk of suffocation. Transition to a sleep sack or a wearable blanket instead.

How do I wash baby blankets?

Most cotton and muslin blankets can be machine-washed in warm water with a gentle, fragrance-free detergent. Avoid fabric softeners, as they can reduce breathability and irritate sensitive skin. Tumble dry on low or hang dry to prevent shrinkage. Always check the care label, especially for organic or specialty blends.

What is a lovey blanket?

A lovey blanket is a small security blanket, usually with a soft plush head attached to a square of fabric. These are not safe for crib use under 12 months due to suffocation and choking risks. They are meant for supervised cuddle time or for toddlers who need a comfort object for sleep once they are old enough.

In shortWhether you go with classic cotton flannel receiving blankets or oversized muslin swaddles, keep safety and practicality front and center. Buy a few high-quality options in breathable, easy-wash fabrics, and rotate them through the laundry so you always have a clean layer ready. The right blanket stash will carry you through spit-ups, middle-of-the-night swaddles, and countless quiet snuggles.
